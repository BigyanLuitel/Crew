Argument in favour of the motion (There need to be strict laws to regulate LLMs)

Thesis: LLMs are powerful, general‑purpose tools that can cause systemic harms at scale; voluntary measures and market incentives alone are insufficient. Clear, enforceable laws are needed to prevent demonstrable public‑interest harms.

Key points
- Public safety and national security: LLMs can produce believable disinformation, facilitate fraud, generate step‑by‑step instructions for cybercrime or dangerous biological/chemical tasks. Laws can prohibit or tightly control outputs and uses that create clear risks.
- Accountability and liability: Without legal obligations, victims of harms (defamation, medical/legal errors, automated discrimination) lack remedies. Laws assign liability, force testing, documentation, and remediation.
- Privacy and data rights: Training data often includes scraped personal or copyrighted material. Legal rules can require consent, provenance auditing, data minimization, and enforceable rights for data subjects.
- Market structure and fairness: Unregulated markets let incumbents hoard compute and proprietary data, concentrate risk, and block competition. Regulation (data‑sharing rules, auditability, anti‑monopoly measures) protects innovation and public interest.
- Standardization and trust: Mandatory disclosures, third‑party audits, and certification create predictable safety baselines that enable responsible adoption across society.
- International coordination: Global risks (election interference, cross‑border harms) require enforceable laws and coordinated enforcement to prevent a race to the bottom.

Argument against the motion (There should not be strict laws to regulate LLMs)

Thesis: Heavy, prescriptive laws enacted now will likely do more harm than good — stifling innovation, entrenching incumbents, and producing brittle rules that quickly become obsolete. Safer paths exist.

Key points
- Innovation chokehold: Broad regulatory compliance costs, licensing, or criminal exposure favor large firms and deter startups and academic research that drive progress and competition.
- Regulatory mismatch and obsolescence: Tech evolves rapidly; prescriptive laws lag and risk banning beneficial applications or failing to address new harms.
- Enforcement and jurisdictional limits: LLM development/deployment is global. National strict laws are hard to enforce internationally and invite regulatory arbitrage.
- Overbreadth and chilling effects: Vague definitions of “harmful” outputs could restrict legitimate uses (education, accessibility, medical tools) and academic freedom.
- Existing legal tools and targeted measures suffice for many harms: Consumer protection, data‑protection regimes (GDPR), export controls for dual‑use tech, criminal law for explicit wrongdoing, and industry standards can address risks without sweeping new statutes.
- Risk of capture and poor design: Rushed, strict laws often reflect incumbent interests or political pressure and create loopholes that fail to protect the public.

Practical compromise (preferred alternative)
- Oppose blanket strict laws in favor of proportionate, risk‑based regulation: mandatory transparency and auditing for high‑risk deployments, targeted prohibitions (e.g., detailed instructions for violent or biochemical wrongdoing), regulatory sandboxes, fast adaptive rulemaking, and international standards/cooperation.
- This balances preventing clear, large‑scale harms while preserving innovation, competition, and legitimate beneficial uses.

Personal stance (as requested: opposing the motion)
- I oppose imposing broad, strict laws now. Favor targeted legal baselines for clearly high‑risk uses combined with adaptive governance, sandboxes, and international coordination to manage risk without freezing innovation.
